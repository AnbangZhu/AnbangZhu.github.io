Speaking of My Childhood Robotics Competitions...

Created2022-12-23|Updated2022-12-27
|Post View:

Hard to believe as it seems now, however, I was a kid that dreamt to become an engineer, a programmer, or an inventer.

Childhood Dreams

Who wasn‚Äôt a Kid?

When I was 4 years old, I first sensed what ‚Äúexcitement‚Äù was at the moment I saw a picture of Lego robots printed on my brother‚Äôs instruction manual of his Lego set though I did not specifically knew how it worked, nor I saw how it was built. The only words that dived in the sea of my mind were that if I could make one of them, it would be the coolest thing in the world. In my fantasy and night dreams, robots could help people do the things that we are tired of, making a less difficult society and of course, defeaing monsters and extra-terrestrials.

So I started with something basic, from putting blocks together to tightening the screws, and I progressed speedily in the first year. Within months, I started to work with motors and wires, growing from a toy player to a builder of something that would work.

I proudly held my first project with electricity: A fish that flaps its fins and make bubble sounds.

Growing Hardcore

Then from some point I needed to programme a robot to make it move. One project that stands out in my mind is building a VEX robot with an arm that could deliver foam toy stars over a barrier, which is a requirement to 2018 World Robot Contest Finals. I was the youngest among our team, almost 5 years younger than the others. I spent my afternoon hours polishing the metalic components, adjusting the gear wheels, and sometimes soldering wires together. Looking back, I was proud that I could work almost incessantly on the projects that are supposed to be ‚Äúadult only.‚Äù I also participated in 5 other robotics competitions, among all of which I ranked the top.

The race set that won me a trophy

I was soldering wires for the set.

Machinery or Humanity?

However, as I grew and began to scrutinize the world around me, I read through human history and began concerned about what took place in the world. It was the moment that I came to comprehend something in advance:there was more to life than machinery and code. I learned that humans are not computers, adhering to binary rules and predictable patterns, nor are we atoms and electrons following predetermined physical laws. We are complex and multifaceted beings, endowed with our own unique thoughts, feelings, and behaviors. The subject matter of the society is conscious sentient beings who act out of choice, not the bots I pieced together that behaved according to electronic signals. Humans are not completely driven by biological instincts. We can only be shaped and partically influenced by culture, institutions, and interactions. It was time to change my life goal. Bots could help, but not decide.

Afterall, more than machinery, we need humanity.
